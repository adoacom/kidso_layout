Rethinking "The Playroom"
CATEGORY:PARTY /DATE:Jan 23, 2015
Share Share Share
As expecting parents, we are ripe with wonderful dreams about what having a child will be like. We anticipate and prepare for our baby in every way possible. Then our babies arrive and we learn that reality and our dreams don't always align. In American culture, we have a concept of "The Playroom". A playroom is a room, in the home, specifically designated and designed for child's play. The concept of a playroom is often one of those warm, fuzzy images expecting parents love to dream about, but the reality of a room filled with toys and children can be more of a headache than we imagine. The following article will debunk the myth of the "catalog" styled playroom and will offer a less expensive and more developmentally appropriate approach to play at home. Maria Montessori (preeminent doctor and child psychoanalyst from the 20th century) found that children learn extensively through interacting with their environment. Therefore, children's environments should be kept orderly and attractive to enable children to be able to use and learn from their living and playspaces. Playrooms stuffed with toys have the potential to be orderly and attractive, but realistically it requires a serious commitment from Mom and Dad to keep the playroom intact. Young children can help to clean up a few toys at time, but cleaning and organizing an entire room full of toys is developmentally inappropriate leaving most of clean-up duty to the adults. Alternatively, allowing a child to have a couple play items in each room of the home helps to space out the chaos and disorder, providing clarity to parent and child. Instead of having twenty-five different toys and all their pieces spread out on the floor (can you picture it!?!), consider having 3-5 toys in each of your main rooms. It is much easier to pick up a couple of toys in a couple rooms, rather than clean and organize an entire disaster area.
